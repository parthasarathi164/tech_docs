Choosing between these two depends on whether you prefer "clean math" or "gritty simulation." However, from a purely technical and workflow perspective, **Topic 1 (Shock-Shock Interaction)** is generally considered the "easier" and more straightforward project.

Here is a breakdown of why Topic 1 is more manageable, along with the hurdles you’ll face in Topic 2.

---

## Topic 1: Shock-Shock Interaction (Parallel Flat Plates)

This is a classic gas dynamics problem. You are essentially looking at how oblique shocks generated by leading edges interact, reflect, or merge.

- **Why it’s easier:**
    
    - **Geometry:** A parallel flat plate configuration is the simplest 2D mesh you can build. No curves, no complex coordinate transformations.
        
    - **Analytical Validation:** You can use **Shock Polar Analysis** or the **Rankine-Hugoniot relations** to predict exactly where the shocks should be and what the pressures should be. There is a "right answer" you can calculate on paper.
        
    - **Physics:** You are dealing with standard compressible flow equations (Euler or Navier-Stokes). Most CFD solvers (like Fluent or OpenFOAM) handle this out of the box with high reliability.
        
- **The Challenge:** The complexity lies in the **Edney Interaction types** (Type I through VI). Depending on the Mach number and plate spacing, the interaction can get messy, but the fundamental physics remain predictable.
    

---

## Topic 2: Porosity and Annular Shock Formation (Axisymmetric)

This topic is significantly more ambitious and involves "compounding complexities."

- **Why it’s harder:**
    
    - **Porous Modeling:** You can't just "turn on" porosity. You have to define permeability ($k$) and inertial resistance coefficients (Darcy-Forchheimer law). Validating these coefficients is a headache unless you have specific experimental data.
        
    - **The "Apples to Oranges" Comparison:** Comparing a supersonic shock-dominated flow with an incompressible boundary layer flow is conceptually difficult. The scaling laws for these two regimes are very different, making a meaningful comparison a massive theoretical undertaking.
        
    - **Numerical Stability:** Shocks are discontinuities. Porous media are "sinks" for momentum. Combining the two often leads to numerical instability at the interface where the shock hits the porous wall, requiring very fine mesh control and specialized solvers.
        
    - **Coordinate System:** While "axisymmetric" isn't hard, it’s still one step more complex than the 2D Cartesian setup of Topic 1.

### Final Verdict

If you want a project where the results are clear and the validation is robust, **go with Topic 1**. It is a bedrock of aerospace engineering and looks great in a portfolio because it demonstrates a deep understanding of gas dynamics.

If you want a project that is more "research-heavy" and you have a lot of time to troubleshoot solver crashes and tune porous resistance parameters, choose **Topic 2**.
