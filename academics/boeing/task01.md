### Design Considerations for the Bench

1. Mounting Plate Clearance

The "Outer Diameter" represents the spinning bell of the motor. When designing your aluminum vertical column or adapter plate, ensure the plate does not exceed this diameter near the face of the motor. If the plate is wider than the bell, it can interfere with the propeller hub or create unnecessary aerodynamic drag in the tractor configuration.

2. Unified Drilling Pattern

All four motors utilize the standard 16 x 19 mm mounting cross. This allows you to manufacture a single set of mounting holes in your AISI 1020 steel or aluminum components that will work for your entire motor testing inventory.

3. Total Top-Mass for CG Calculation

When using the AIR 2216 II, your "top-heavy" mass increases to approximately 64 g. In your Python stability script, use this as the primary value for the motor component to ensure your Factor of Safety (FoS) remains above 2.0 even with your heaviest motor.

4. Shaft Interference

While the shaft diameter varies (3.17 mm to 5.0 mm), most of these motors have shafts that protrude from the back as well. Ensure your motor mounting plate has a central "clearance hole" (typically 8–10 mm) to allow the back of the motor shaft and the C-clip to rotate freely without touching the plate.

## Force Transfer Rod

### Technical Justification: Force Transfer Rod Integration

- **Axial Load Isolation**: The rod-and-bearing assembly decouples pure thrust from the parasitic torque, bending moments, and gyroscopic forces inherently generated by 5–10 inch propellers.
    
- **Measurement Precision**: By constraining the assembly to a single degree of freedom, the system ensures the load cell reacts only to axial forces, which minimizes thrust sensitivity errors across the four specified motors.
    
- **Component Protection**: The housing absorbs off-axis fatigue and impact loads, protecting the load cell's delicate strain gauges from damage at the 2.25kg limit load (1.5 Factor of Safety).

### **Technical Selection Report: Linear Guidance Sub-system**

#### **1. Component Specification**

To ensure compatibility with standard industrial components and meet your precision requirements, the following specifications are recommended:

- **Linear Bearing:** **LM8UU** Linear Ball Bearing.
    
    - _Dimensions:_ Inner Diameter (ID) $8\text{ mm}$, Outer Diameter (OD) $15\text{ mm}$, Length $24\text{ mm}$.
        
- **Force Transfer Rod:** **Case-Hardened Steel (CF53)** or **Hardened Stainless Steel (440C)**.
    
    - _Dimensions:_ Diameter $8\text{ mm}$ ($h6$ tolerance), Length $50\text{ mm}$.
        
- **Housing Adjustment:** Increase the housing bore from $12.5\text{ mm}$ to **$15\text{ mm}$** to allow for a press-fit or snap-ring installation of the LM8UU.
    

---

#### **2. Material and Hardware Justification**

**A. Why Hardened Steel over AL 6061?**

Linear ball bearings use recirculating steel balls that create high-point contact stress. Aluminum 6061-T6 has a Brinell hardness of approximately $95\text{ HB}$, which is insufficient to resist the $58\text{-}62\text{ HRC}$ hardness of the bearing balls. Using aluminum would result in **Brinelling** (permanent grooving of the rod), causing the system to seize and introducing massive measurement errors.

**B. Why the LM8UU (8 mm) over the 7.5 mm custom rod?**

Standardization is key for precision. Linear bearings are manufactured to match specific metric rod tolerances (typically $h6$). A $7.5\text{ mm}$ rod would have excessive "slop" or play in a standard bearing, leading to vibration during $10$-inch propeller tests. An $8\text{ mm}$ system provides the rigidity needed for a $1.5\text{ kg}$ thrust load.

---

#### **3. Mathematical Verification**

**A. Load Calculations**

- **Maximum Thrust ($F_{max}$):** $1.5\text{ kg} \times 9.81\text{ m/s}^2 = 14.715\text{ N}$.
    
- **Design Load ($F_{design}$):** $14.715\text{ N} \times 1.5\text{ (FOS)} = 22.07\text{ N}$.
    
- **Bearing Capacity:** A standard LM8UU has a Dynamic Load Rating ($C$) of approximately $260\text{ N}$.
    
    - **Safety Margin:** $\frac{260\text{ N}}{22.07\text{ N}} \approx 11.7$. The bearing is well within its operating limits, ensuring long-term reliability.
        

**B. Friction and Sensitivity Error**

To meet your requirement of minimizing thrust sensitivity error, we calculate the frictional resistance ($F_f$):

$$F_f = \mu \times F_{normal}$$

Using a conservative coefficient of friction ($\mu$) for linear ball bearings of $0.005$ and assuming the worst-case normal load (weight of the motor/plate assembly $\approx 200\text{ g}$ or $1.96\text{ N}$):

$$F_f = 0.005 \times 1.96\text{ N} = 0.0098\text{ N}$$

- **Total Measurement Error contribution from friction:**
    
    $$\text{Error} \% = \left( \frac{0.0098\text{ N}}{14.715\text{ N}} \right) \times 100 \approx 0.066\%$$
    
    This negligible error confirms the efficiency of the linear ball bearing approach for high-precision thrust measurement.

## Linear Ball Bearing

### **Technical Justification: Selection of LM8UU Linear Ball Bearing**

For a high-precision thrust measurement system, the **LM8UU linear ball bearing** is selected over alternative bearing types (such as radial ball bearings or plain bushings) for the following reasons:

- **Pure Axial Motion Support:** Unlike standard radial ball bearings designed for rotation, the LM8UU is specifically engineered for linear translation. Its recirculating ball circuits allow the **AISI 1045/440C rod** to move with near-zero resistance along the thrust axis, ensuring that the 1.5 kg thrust is transmitted accurately to the load cell without mechanical binding.
    
- **Minimal Hysteresis (Thrust Sensitivity):** To minimize thrust sensitivity error, the mechanical interface must have extremely low static and dynamic friction. The point-contact nature of the recirculating balls provides a significantly lower coefficient of friction ($\mu \approx 0.001 \text{ to } 0.005$) compared to plain bushings (bronze or plastic), preventing "stick-slip" effects that would otherwise mask small changes in thrust.
    
- **Vibrational Damping and Radial Rigidity:** The tight tolerance fit (typically $h6/g6$ compatibility) of the LM8UU ensures the **8 mm rod** is radially constrained. This is critical for stability when testing **10-inch propellers**, as it prevents the rod from vibrating or "chattering" within the housing, which would introduce high-frequency noise into the load cell signal.
    
- **Standardization and Integration:** The LM8UU is an industrial standard, offering a predictable form factor ($15\text{ mm}$ OD, $24\text{ mm}$ Length) that integrates easily into the **AL 6061 bearing housing**. This ensures the design remains "as light as possible" by avoiding the need for heavy custom housings or complex multi-bearing arrays required by other linear guidance methods.

## ESC and Sensors

### 1. The "Protection" Misconception

First, a quick technical correction: a Voltage/Current (V/I) sensor does **not** protect the ESC. A sensor only passively measures what is happening.

- **Protection** is achieved through component sizing (which we did by selecting a 40A+ ESC for a maximum 30A load) and by placing a physical fuse or current-limiting power supply between the battery and the ESC.
    

### 2. RPM Measurement: Optical vs. Electronic (ESC)

You are absolutely correct that relying on the ESC for RPM introduces a philosophical and technical conflict in a testing environment.

- **Electronic RPM (ESC Telemetry):** The ESC calculates RPM by reading the "Back-EMF" (voltage generated by the spinning magnets) to know when to fire the next electrical phase. It divides this electrical frequency by the number of motor poles to guess the mechanical RPM.
    
    - _The Flaw:_ It is an inferred calculation, not a physical measurement. Furthermore, you are relying on the actuator (the ESC) to report its own performance. In aerospace engineering, this is known as a lack of independent verification.
        
- **Optical RPM (Infrared/Laser):** This physically counts the propeller blades breaking a beam or reflecting light.
    
    - _The Advantage:_ It establishes an **Independent Physical Truth**. It measures the actual mechanical output at the propeller, capturing any slip, physical deformation, or desynchronization that the electrical signal might miss.
        

### 3. Voltage & Current: Dedicated Sensors vs. ESC

While ESC telemetry is convenient for hobbyists, it falls short for professional data acquisition.

- **ESC Telemetry:** The internal shunts and voltage dividers in most ESCs are cheap, uncalibrated, and often have an error margin of 2% to 5%.
    
- **Dedicated V/I Sensors:** Standalone sensors (like an INA226-based module or a calibrated Hall-effect current sensor) utilize high-resolution Analog-to-Digital Converters (ADCs).
    
    - _The Advantage:_ They can be physically isolated from the electrically "noisy" ESC. More importantly, you can independently calibrate a dedicated sensor with a known lab power supply, which is a strict requirement for validating your test bench under the ARP4754 process.
        

---

### The Verdict: What is the BEST Option?

Given that your primary goal is high fidelity, traceable data, and a rigorous engineering journey, **your intuition is correct. You should abandon the "all-in-one" ESC telemetry approach and use an Independent Sensor Architecture.**

Here is the best path forward for your documentation and design:

**1. Motor Control:** Use a high-quality, standard 40A-50A ESC (BLHeli_32 is still good for smooth signal processing, but we will ignore its telemetry).

**2. RPM Acquisition:** Use an **Optical/IR Reflective Sensor**. Mount it securely to the vertical AL 6061 column, pointing at the rear of the motor hub (paint one half of the hub white, the other black for a clean signal). This provides undeniable, independent mechanical RPM data.

**3. Power Acquisition:** Install a **Dedicated High-Precision V/I Sensor** (e.g., a calibrated shunt resistor board) inline between the battery and the ESC. This provides verifiable electrical input data.

**The Justification Summary:**

> "To comply with rigorous validation standards, an Independent Sensor Architecture was selected over integrated ESC telemetry. Dedicated optical RPM and high-resolution V/I sensors separate the measurement instruments from the device under test (the ESC/Motor). This ensures that all mechanical output and electrical input data are independently verifiable and can be individually calibrated, eliminating the self-reporting bias inherent to integrated motor controllers."

### Technical Justification: ESC Current Rating Selection

- **Operational Safety Margin:** The highest current draw in the test matrix is approximately **30A** (Emax RS 2205 with 5-inch prop). A **40A ESC** provides a 33% safety overhead ($FOS \approx 1.33$), ensuring the controller operates within its reliable nominal range rather than at its absolute thermal limit.
    
- **Thermal Stability and Data Integrity:** Power loss and heat generation follow the formula $P = I^2 R$. A 40A ESC typically utilizes MOSFETs with lower internal resistance ($R_{ds(on)}$) than a 30A model. This reduces heat buildup during testing, preventing "thermal throttling" which would otherwise cause inconsistent power delivery and introduce errors in thrust measurements.
    
- **Steady-State Duty Cycle:** Test bench protocols require sustained full-throttle runs to achieve stable data, unlike flight where maximum current is usually a transient "burst." The 40A rating ensures the ESC can handle **100% duty cycle** loads for extended durations without risk of catastrophic MOSFET failure or capacitor degradation.

## Potentiometer

### Design Rationale: Physical Potentiometer for Throttle Control

While software-based control offers high repeatability, a physical potentiometer is integrated into the test bench architecture for the following technical reasons:

- **Zero-Latency Emergency Override:** A potentiometer provides a direct, analog hardware-in-the-loop (HITL) safety path. In the event of a software hang, serial communication lag, or mechanical failure (e.g., propeller delamination), the operator can instantly kill the throttle without relying on computer processing.
    
- **Tactile Resonance Scouting:** During initial test phases, a physical dial allows for precise manual "sweeping" of the RPM range. This is essential for identifying specific mechanical resonance frequencies in the **AL 6061** vertical column and **I-base stand** that might not be captured in a stepped software script.
    
- **System Decoupling and Diagnostics:** Separating the control input from the data acquisition computer ensures that the motor remains controllable even if the PC or microcontroller’s serial buffer becomes overloaded. This independence is critical for verifying the ESC’s endpoint calibration and neutral response zones before initiating automated test sequences.

## Power Supply

### **Power Source Rationale: LiPo Battery Selection**

To maintain a focus on high-fidelity performance data and system simplicity, a **3S (11.1V) 3300mAh 45C LiPo battery** is selected as the exclusive power source for this test bench.

- **Operational Fidelity (Voltage Sag):** Unlike a "stiff" regulated power supply, a LiPo battery possesses internal resistance that naturally causes voltage sag under load. Testing with a battery captures the real-world performance decay a motor will experience in flight, providing more accurate discharge-to-thrust data than a constant-voltage source.
    
- **Transient Load Handling:** Brushless ESCs draw current in extremely fast pulses. Chemical batteries can supply these instantaneous high-current spikes (up to **30A** for the RS 2205) more efficiently than many regulated bench supplies, which may suffer from transient response lags or trip protective circuits during rapid throttle changes.
    
- **Signal Integrity and Noise Reduction:** Regulated switching power supplies often introduce high-frequency electrical ripple or "noise" into the system. Using a battery provides a clean, isolated DC reference, which is critical for protecting the precision of the **load cell** and **optical RPM** signal data from electromagnetic interference (EMI).
    
- **Safety and Portability:** A battery allows the test bench to remain "electrically floating," reducing the risk of ground loops between the bench and the computer. Additionally, it eliminates the need for bulky, expensive high-current power cables, keeping the **I-base stand** assembly light and mobile as required.
